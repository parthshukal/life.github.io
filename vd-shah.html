I’m horrible at arithmetic. Shall I enroll in the program?

No! Finance is synonymous with numbers. That doesn’t mean complex stats, but logical calculations of fractions percentages and problem-solving.

For example, you’re studying probability concepts. You come across a formula:

Probability of (A or B) = P(A) +P(B) - P(A&B).

Where does the -P(A&B) come from in the formula?

As an analyst you can think like this: Out of four days, A comes to class for two days (50% of the times), and B comes for two days (50%). Can I say that on all four days (100% of days), either A or B will be present?

No. Why not? Because A & B might turn up on the same day. A comes on 1st & 2nd. B comes on the 2nd & 3rd day. When I add A + B, we end up counting Day 2 twice. To correct for the double counting, we deduct (A&B - the day they both came).

Example 2) When we add 1/2 + 1/2, why it doesn't equal 2/4?

Half indicates one of two pieces. If you have a full pizza, cut it into two pieces and take one, you have half a pizza.

Imagine you’re at a pizza party and there are many large pizza slices on the table. (1 slice = half of a pizza)

In a fraction, the numerator indicates the number of pieces you have picked up and the denominator indicates the number of pieces that make a full pizza (2 in this case).

When you take 2 such slices, your numerator becomes 2. The denominator is the number of slices that make up a full pizza. Since we require 2 such slices to make up a full pizza, the denominator will remain 2. Hence, we don't add the denominator.
